A Hunza tour from Islamabad is Pakistan's classic mountain journey: roughly 600 kilometres up the Karakoram Highway to a valley ringed by 7,000-metre peaks, turquoise Attabad Lake, and some of the friendliest communities in the country. ## Why Hunza Is Worth the Drive

Hunza rewards patience. The drive from Islamabad is long — most groups split it over a night in Besham or Chilas — but the payoff starts before you arrive: Rakaposhi (7,788 m) appears beside the highway near Ghulmet, close enough that you can stop for tea directly facing the mountain. Once in the valley, distances shrink. Karimabad, Altit, Attabad Lake, Gulmit and Passu all sit within comfortable day-trip range of each other, so after the travel days the trip feels relaxed rather than rushed.

Hunza is also one of the easiest parts of Gilgit-Baltistan for first-timers. Literacy is high, tourism is well established, families and solo female travellers are welcomed, and local food — chapshuro, harissa, apricot everything — is a highlight in itself.

## The Route from Islamabad (Day by Day)

**Leg 1 — Islamabad to Besham or Chilas.** Departure is typically early morning or overnight. The route runs Hazara Motorway (M-15) to Thakot, then joins the Karakoram Highway. Expect winding sections after Besham where the road follows the Indus gorge.

**Leg 2 — Chilas to Hunza.** The most scenic driving day in Pakistan. You pass the Nanga Parbat viewpoint near Raikot, the Three Mountain Ranges junction at Jaglot (where the Karakoram, Himalaya and Hindu Kush meet), and Rakaposhi viewpoint before climbing into central Hunza.

**In the valley.** Typical inclusions on our departures: Karimabad bazaar, Baltit and Altit forts, Attabad Lake boating, the Hussaini suspension bridge, Passu Cones, and — season and border conditions permitting — Khunjerab Pass at 4,693 m. Exact itinerary varies by package; check the live itinerary on this page.

**Return.** Some seasonal departures return via Babusar Top and Naran when the pass is open (usually late June to late September — see our [Babusar opening dates guide]), turning the trip into a loop.

## Top Places You'll Visit

**Attabad Lake** — formed by the 2010 landslide; boating on unreal blue water. **Baltit Fort** — 700-year-old fort above Karimabad with Ultar Sar behind it. **Eagle's Nest (Duikar)** — sunrise point over the whole valley. **Passu Cones** — the jagged Cathedral Ridge, best photographed from the KKH. **Khunjerab Pass** — the Pak-China border, the world's highest paved border crossing; carry your original CNIC (or passport for foreign visitors).

## Best Time to Visit Hunza

- **Late March–April:** Cherry and apricot blossom. Cool, occasionally wet, magical. Books out earliest — see our [cherry blossom guide].
- **May–June:** Green valley, snow still on peaks, ideal weather. Peak family season.
- **July–August:** Warm days, all attractions open, Khunjerab reliably accessible. Busiest period; monsoon barely touches Hunza but can affect the highway south of Chilas.
- **October:** Autumn gold and red across the valley — many regulars' favourite month.
- **November–February:** Cold, quiet, deeply discounted, and stunning on clear days. Khunjerab often snow-restricted; some hotels close. Winter Hunza suits couples and photographers more than families with small kids.

## How to Reach & Road Conditions

From Islamabad the practical option is road; the alternative is flying to Gilgit, but flights are weather-dependent and frequently rescheduled, so organized tours run by road for reliability. The KKH is fully paved but conditions vary by section: the Besham–Dasu stretch has ongoing works around the Dasu dam project and occasional single-lane sections; the Indus Kohistan gorge sees rockfall after heavy rain; north of Chilas the road is generally excellent. We update our [Karakoram Highway conditions page] quarterly and brief every group before departure. Night driving on the gorge sections is avoided on our tours.

## Weather by Season

Summer days in central Hunza sit around 25–30°C with cool evenings; spring and autumn range roughly 10–20°C daytime; winter days often stay below 10°C with nights well below freezing. Khunjerab is cold year-round — even in July, expect near-freezing wind chill at the top.

## Travel Tips

Carry cash; ATMs exist in Aliabad and Karimabad but can run dry on peak weekends. SCOM SIMs work best in Gilgit-Baltistan — other networks are patchy beyond Gilgit city. Keep your CNIC accessible for the checkposts along the KKH. Sunday markets and fort visits are best done early before day-trip crowds arrive.

## Budget Tips

The biggest cost drivers on any Hunza trip are transport type (coaster vs private car), hotel category, and season — blossom season and Eid weeks price highest. Travelling in shared group departures, choosing shoulder months (May, October), and booking early for peak windows are the honest ways to save. Current package pricing is always on this page's booking section; we don't quote prices in articles because they change with fuel costs and season.

## Photography Tips

Golden hour at Eagle's Nest is the classic shot — arrive 40 minutes before sunrise. Rakaposhi viewpoint works best in morning light. For Passu Cones, shoot from the KKH south of Passu village in late afternoon. Attabad's colour is strongest under midday sun. Bring a polarizing filter; the light at altitude is harsh.

## Family Suitability

**Excellent for families.** Driving days are long but hotels, food and washroom stops on this route are the best-established in the north. Attabad boating, the forts and the suspension bridge keep kids engaged. Khunjerab's altitude (4,693 m) can cause headaches in young children and elderly travellers — the visit is short, and skipping it is always an option. Winter departures are better for adults than toddlers.

## Packing List

Layered clothing in every season (warm jacket even in July for Khunjerab), sunblock and sunglasses (high-altitude sun is intense), power bank, cash, CNIC/passport, personal medicines, motion-sickness tablets for gorge sections, and a warm cap. Winter adds thermals, gloves and proper insulated footwear. Full details in our [northern areas packing guide].

## Things to Avoid

Don't plan Khunjerab as a guaranteed item in early spring or late autumn — snow closes it without notice. Don't rely on card payments. Don't photograph checkposts or military installations. Don't schedule your return with zero buffer before a flight or exam — the KKH occasionally closes for hours after slides, and a smart itinerary absorbs that.

## FAQs

1. **How far is Hunza from Islamabad?** Around 600 km via the Karakoram Highway; organized tours split the journey with an overnight stop en route.
2. **How many days do I need for a Hunza tour from Islamabad?** Five to seven days is the comfortable standard — shorter trips spend too high a share of time on the road.
3. **Is the road to Hunza safe?** The KKH is paved and heavily used; the main risks are seasonal rockfall zones in Kohistan, which is why our departures avoid night driving there and monitor NHA alerts.
4. **Can I visit Khunjerab Pass on this tour?** Yes when weather and border conditions allow, typically May–October. Carry your original CNIC or passport.
5. **Is Hunza suitable for a honeymoon?** Very — especially blossom season and October. See our honeymoon packages for couple-focused departures.
6. **Do mobile networks work in Hunza?** SCOM works best; Zong/Jazz coverage is limited beyond Gilgit. Most hotels have Wi-Fi of varying quality.
7. **Is Hunza safe for solo female travellers?** Hunza is widely regarded as one of the most welcoming districts in Pakistan for women travelling alone or in groups.
8. **What food will I find?** Local Hunza cuisine (chapshuro, dowdo soup, apricot dishes) plus standard Pakistani menus at hotels.
9. **Are there ATMs in Hunza?** Yes in Aliabad and Karimabad, but carry sufficient cash — machines run out on busy weekends.
10. **Can elderly parents manage this trip?** Generally yes; the long drives and Khunjerab's altitude are the two things to plan around. Tell us at booking and we'll advise.
11. **When is cherry blossom season?** Usually late March to mid-April, varying year to year with weather.
12. **Does the tour return via Babusar Top?** On seasonal loop departures when the pass is open (roughly late June–late September); otherwise return is via the KKH.
13. **Do I need a permit for Hunza?** Pakistani citizens need only a CNIC. Foreign tourists currently travel Hunza/KKH without special permits, but requirements can change — we confirm current rules at booking.

## Introduction

Sharaan Forest (often written Sharan) is a dense pine and fir forest above the village of Paras in Kaghan Valley — close enough to Islamabad for a long day trip, remote enough that you still need a 4x4 jeep for the final climb. If you've seen those misty green photos that look like northern Europe and wondered whether they're really in Pakistan: yes, and this is the place. This guide explains the route, the jeep track, timing, what the forest offers, and — because it's the question everyone asks — how jeep charges work.

## What Makes Sharaan Special

Most Kaghan destinations are about open valleys and rivers. Sharaan is the opposite: a closed-canopy forest sitting above 2,400 metres, thick with moss, ferns and towering conifers, often wrapped in mist by afternoon. A forest rest house and marked walking trails sit inside it, and the Manshi Top trek starts from here for those who want to go further. It photographs like nowhere else within day-trip range of Islamabad.

## Route from Islamabad

The drive runs Islamabad → Hazara Motorway → Mansehra → Balakot → Paras, roughly 4–4.5 hours in normal traffic. Paras is a small Kaghan Valley town on the Kunhar River; this is where vehicles are parked and the jeep stage begins. Day trips leave Islamabad early — typically before sunrise — to protect forest time.

## The Jeep Track from Paras

The track from Paras to Sharaan climbs about 12–14 km of steep, unpaved switchbacks and takes roughly 1 to 1.5 hours each way in a local 4x4. It is a genuine jeep track: narrow in places, rough after rain, driven by local drivers who know it well. Ordinary cars cannot make this climb, which is exactly why the forest has stayed quiet. Jeeps are hired in Paras and, as noted above, **jeep charges are paid locally and are not part of the package** — this is standard across Kaghan's jeep destinations because rates are set by local jeep associations and vary by season.

## What to Do at the Top

Walk the forest trails (flat to gently rolling near the rest house), picnic in the clearings, photograph the mist rolling through the trees, and — if your group has energy and daylight — hike a portion of the Manshi Top trail for meadow views above the treeline. Overnight camping trips to Sharaan are also possible; ask us about camping departures if a single day feels short.

## Best Time to Visit

**April to June** offers the freshest green and most reliable track conditions. **July–August** brings dramatic mist but also monsoon rain, which can make the track slippery and occasionally close it for a day — build flexibility in. **September–October** is crisp and clear. **Winter** snow generally closes the jeep track; Sharaan is effectively a summer-half destination.

## Road Conditions & Weather

The road is excellent motorway and highway to Balakot, good paved road to Paras, then unpaved jeep track. After heavy rain the Kaghan road can face temporary blockages near known slide points; we check conditions the evening before every departure. Forest temperatures run 8–10°C cooler than Islamabad — pleasant in summer days, genuinely cold by evening even in June.

## Travel Tips

Leave Islamabad by 5 a.m.; the day works on early margins. Eat a proper breakfast in Balakot or carry it — options at Paras are basic. Mobile signal is weak to absent in the forest; download offline maps. Keep your CNIC with you.

## Budget Tips

The main variable costs on this trip are the jeep (shared between the group — fuller jeeps mean lower per-head cost) and food. Joining a group departure rather than going private keeps both transport and jeep sharing efficient. Current day-trip pricing is on this page's booking section.

## Photography Tips

Mist usually builds from late morning — the moody forest shots happen between about 11 a.m. and 3 p.m. in summer. Shoot into the canopy with a wide lens; expose for the highlights in fog. The jeep track itself offers big Kunhar Valley views on the way up — sit on the open side and keep your camera out.

## Family Suitability

Good for families with children roughly 6 and up who can handle a bumpy jeep ride; the forest walking itself is easy. Not ideal for infants or anyone with severe back problems (the track is rough). Elderly travellers who are comfortable in 4x4s manage it well — the destination itself requires little walking.

## Packing List

Warm layer even in summer, rain jacket (always, in Kaghan), grippy shoes, cash for jeep and food, power bank, snacks and water, sunblock, and a dry bag or plastic covers for electronics in monsoon months.

## Things to Avoid

Don't attempt the track in your own sedan — it damages cars and blocks the route for others. Don't litter; Sharaan is pristine because visitors have mostly kept it that way. Don't push for the trip on a day of active heavy rain — we reschedule rather than gamble on a slippery track. Don't expect restaurants at the top; food is a carry-in affair.

## FAQs

1. **Where is Sharaan Forest?** Above Paras village in Kaghan Valley, KPK — about 4.5 hours' drive from Islamabad plus a jeep climb.
2. **Are jeep charges included in the package?** No. Jeep charges are paid locally to Paras jeep operators and are not included, as rates are set locally and vary by season. We guide every group on current arrangements.
3. **How long is the jeep ride?** Roughly 1–1.5 hours each way on a steep unpaved track.
4. **Can I take my own car to Sharaan?** Only to Paras. The track beyond requires a local 4x4.
5. **Is one day enough?** Yes for the forest and short walks; choose an overnight camping option for Manshi Top or a slower pace.
6. **Is Sharaan open in winter?** The track is usually snow-closed in winter; plan for April–October.
7. **Is it safe in monsoon?** Trips run in monsoon but with weather checks; we reschedule if the track is unsafe after heavy rain.
8. **Is there mobile signal?** Weak to none in the forest — treat it as offline time.
9. **Are there washrooms?** Basic facilities near the rest house area; standards are rustic.
10. **Is it suitable for kids?** Yes from around age six, mainly due to the bumpy jeep ride rather than the forest itself.
11. **Can we camp overnight?** Yes — ask about camping departures; nights are cold even in summer, and gear matters.
12. **What should we eat?** Carry lunch/snacks; last proper food stops are Balakot and basic options in Paras.
